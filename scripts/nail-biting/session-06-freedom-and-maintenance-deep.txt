[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Second: the wrist tap. Whenever you notice your hand moving toward your mouth, two taps on your wrist. This interrupts the pattern, brings awareness, creates a pause for choosing differently.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Third: the competing response. Gentle fists, held for one to two minutes or until the urge passes. Physical impossibility of biting plus time for urges to fade.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Fourth: emotional awareness. When urges arise, ask: what am I feeling? Then address the feeling directly. Breathing for anxiety. Engagement for boredom. Expression for frustration. Connection for sadness.

--- Maintenance Practices ---

Now let us establish practices that will maintain your freedom for life.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

First: nail care. Keep your nails properly maintained. Trim them regularly to a comfortable length. File any rough edges that might tempt you to "fix" them with your teeth. Moisturize cuticles to keep them healthy. When nails are well-cared-for, they are easier to leave alone.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Carry a small nail file with you. Keep one at home, at work, in your car. When you notice a rough edge, fix it properly—not with your teeth.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Second: stress management. High stress is when old habits most want to return. Develop ongoing practices for managing stress. Exercise. Prayer. Connection with loved ones. Deep breathing. Whatever works for you—use it regularly, not just in crisis.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Third: emotional awareness. Continue noticing your emotions. The habit of checking in with yourself—"What am I feeling?"—serves you well beyond nail biting. It is a life skill.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Fourth: periodic reinforcement. Return to these sessions from time to time. Listen to them as refreshers, especially if you notice old patterns trying to resurface. The neural pathways of freedom strengthen with continued use.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Fifth: self-compassion. Be kind to yourself throughout this process. Progress matters more than perfection. If you ever experience a slip, respond with curiosity rather than condemnation. What triggered it? What will you do differently? Learn and move forward.

[PAUSE 6 SECONDS]

--- Handling Slips ---

Let us address the possibility of slips directly.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

A slip is not a failure. It is not "starting over." It is simply information—a data point about what triggered you and what you can strengthen.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

If you ever find yourself biting—or having bitten—before you caught it, do not despair. Do not condemn yourself. Do not think "I'm back to square one" or "This doesn't work."

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Instead, get curious. What was happening? What were you feeling? What situation were you in? The answers help you prepare better for similar situations in the future.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Then recommit. Use your anchor. Say your identity statement: I am someone who does not bite their nails. A slip does not change your identity—it just means you had a moment of forgetting. The identity remains.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

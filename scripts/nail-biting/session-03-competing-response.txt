today you learn the competing response. This is one of the most powerful techniques in Habit Reversal Training, backed by decades of research showing its effectiveness for body-focused repetitive behaviors.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

The competing response is a specific physical action that you do instead of nail biting when you notice the urge. It is a replacement behavior, one that is incompatible with biting because it occupies the same muscles in a different way.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

The competing response we will install today is simple but powerful: when you notice an urge to bite your nails, you will make a gentle fist and hold it for sixty to ninety seconds.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

Let me explain why this works. When you make a fist, several things happen. First, your fingers are enclosed, making it physically impossible to bite them. Second, the muscles involved are occupied, redirecting the nervous energy. Third, you are taking conscious control of your hands.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

The fist does not need to be tight or tense. In fact, a gentle fist is better. Just close your fingers into your palm comfortably and hold them there. You can do this subtly, almost unnoticed by others.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

In your imagination now, practice this. Picture yourself noticing an urge to bite. You feel that familiar pull. And immediately, smoothly, naturally, you close your hand into a gentle fist.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Feel the position of your hand. Your fingers curled into your palm. Your thumb resting outside or tucked comfortably. Hold this position.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

As you hold the fist, the urge begins to change. It may intensify briefly, but then it peaks and starts to fade. Like a wave, it rises, crests, and falls. And all the while, your hand remains in the fist, occupied, protected.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

After sixty to ninety seconds, the urge has typically passed. You can release the fist, and your hand returns to normal. But now, instead of bitten nails, you have intact nails. You have won this small battle.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

This competing response is now being programmed into your subconscious mind, Austin. It is becoming automatic. When urge arises, fist forms. This connection is being wired deeply into your neural pathways.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Let us strengthen this programming with repetition. Imagine another scenario. You are sitting at your desk, working. Your mind wanders, and you notice your hand drifting toward your mouth.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

Immediately, automatically, you recognize the urge. And your hand, instead of continuing to your mouth, closes into a gentle fist. You hold it there, resting on your desk or in your lap. The urge passes. Your nails are safe.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Another scenario. You are watching something on a screen. It's somewhat boring, and your hands are idle. The familiar urge arises.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

You notice. You recognize. And smoothly, naturally, your hand forms the competing response. A gentle fist. You hold it while watching. The urge fades. You are in control.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

One more. You are feeling stressed about something. Anxiety is present. Your fingers feel restless, seeking something to do.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

You notice the emotional trigger. You notice the urge building. And before your hand reaches your mouth, it transforms into a fist. You are redirecting the nervous energy. You are caring for your hands instead of damaging them.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

this competing response will become as automatic as the old habit once was. It simply takes practice and repetition. Each time you use it, it becomes stronger. Each time you successfully redirect an urge, the new pathway deepens.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

The Apostle Paul spoke of putting off the old man and putting on the new. This is exactly what you are doing. The old pattern of nail biting is being put off. The new pattern of the competing response is being put on. You are becoming a new creation.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Now, let us add another layer to your toolkit. Sometimes the urge may be particularly strong, or you may be in a situation where a fist is awkward. In these cases, you can use an alternative competing response.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

The alternative is to press your fingertips firmly against your thumb on the same hand, creating a circle or pressed shape. This also occupies the muscles and keeps your fingers away from your mouth.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Or, you can press your hands flat against a surface, a table, your thighs, the arms of a chair. The key is that your hands are occupied and your fingers are protected.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

You now have multiple options: the gentle fist, the finger press, or the flat hand press. Your subconscious mind will choose the most appropriate one for each situation.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Let us program a positive reward into this system. Each time you successfully use the competing response and an urge passes without biting, you will feel a small surge of satisfaction. A quiet sense of victory.

[PAUSE 3 SECONDS]

This positive feeling reinforces the new behavior. Your brain learns: competing response equals good feeling. Over time, using the competing response becomes rewarding in itself.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Imagine now, that it is a few weeks from today. You have been using the competing response consistently. Look at your hands in your imagination. See how the nails have begun to grow out. See the healthy nail beds, the smooth edges.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

Feel the pride you experience looking at your hands. The relief of not hiding them anymore. The confidence of knowing you have overcome this habit. This is your future, and the competing response is helping you create it.

[PAUSE 5 SECONDS]

Remember, the sequence is: awareness, then competing response. First you notice the urge or the trigger. Then you implement the fist or alternative. The awareness you developed in the last session combines with the competing response you learned today.

[PAUSE 4 SECONDS]

You are building a complete system for change. Each piece supports the others. Awareness catches the urge early. The competing response redirects it. And with each success, the old habit weakens while the new pattern strengthens.

The Lord has given you agency, the power to choose. The competing response is how you exercise that agency over this habit. You are not a victim of automatic behavior. You are a conscious being with the power to choose differently.

Practice the competing response a few times each day, even when there is no urge. Simply make the fist and hold it for a minute. This keeps the response fresh and ready, like a tool you keep sharp for when you need it.
